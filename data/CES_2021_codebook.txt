We aimed for 50% men and 50% women. We aimed to have 28% of our respondents aged 18-34, 33% aged 35-54, and 39% aged 55 and higher. The regions were: Atlantic (Newfoundland and Labrador, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Prince Edward Island), Quebec, Ontario, West (Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta, British Columbia), and Territories (Northwest Territories, Nunavut, Yukon). We set quotas for Atlantic, Quebec, Ontario and West that mimic their distribution in the population (7%, 23%, 38%, and 32%, respectively) but accepted any respondents from the Northwest Territories, Nunavut or Yukon. We also aimed to have 80% French and 20% English within Quebec, 10% French within the Atlantic region, and 10% French nationally. Respondents needed to be aged 18 or over and Canadian citizens or permanent residents in order to participate.   The CPS had three waves, “CPS”, “CPS Modules”, and “CPS Oversample” which were merged together, resulting in the final sample of 20,968. The “CPS” wave soft launched on August 17, fully launched on August 18, and ran until September 8, resulting in a sample of 5820. The “CPS Modules” wave ran from September 9 to September 19, 2021 and resulted in a sample of 13,120. The “CPS Modules” wave contained the same questions as the “CPS”, plus several additional question modules (see Additional question modules below). The “CPS Oversample” wave ran from August 18 to September 19, 2021. The “CPS Oversample” resulted in a sample of 2028. The “CPS Oversample” did not have sampling quotas and contained identical survey questions to the “CPS” wave. The “Oversample” wave collected overflow responses from full quotas in the “CPS” and “CPS Modules”.   We initially aimed for a daily sample of 300 respondents for the campaign period. During the “CPS Modules” wave, which occurred in the last 10 days of the campaign, we increased our target to 1500 respondents per day. The target number was increased 2  during the final 10 days to increase the total number of respondents and provide enough responses for the additional question modules. These targets were not always achieved.    Sampling for the Campaign Period Survey occurred from August 17 to September 19, 2021. The survey instrument was presented on the Qualtrics online platform.   Sample design of Post-Election Survey (PES)   All respondents from the CPS were re-contacted after the election for a follow-up survey. 15,069 respondents completed the PES (return rate of 72%). Sampling for the PES occurred from September 23 to October 4, 2021. The survey instrument was presented on the Qualtrics online platform.  Panel provider  All sample was provided by Leger Opinion.   	Survey weights   Weights have been created for the dataset using an iterative "raking'' process, as provided by the ipfraking command in STATA15. Marginal values were successively weighted according to province, as well as gender, age group, and education level. All population data were taken from the 2016 Canadian census. A maximum of 200 iterations were completed. The dataset includes the weights produced as 4 variables, depending on which portion of the sample is being used:  1. cps21_weight_general_all - all campaign period respondents  2. cps21_weight_general_restricted - only high-quality campaign period respondents (see Data Quality section below)  3. pes21_weight_general_all - all post-election survey respondents  4. pes21_weight_general_restricted - only high-quality post-election survey respondents   Note that respondents from the Territories do not have weights, as they were not included in the sampling frame, because data collection in the Territories is too sparse to be representative.   Data Quality   Responses flagged with the following data quality concerns were removed from the dataset, and are excluded from numbers reported in this codebook: • Incomplete responses • Duplicate response of previous respondents  • Speeders • Respondents that “straight-lined” grid questions (“straightliners”) • Respondents whose postal code did not match their province  • “YOB mismatch” respondents, who provided a different year-of-birth at the beginning of the CPS compared to the end of the CPS. As a quality check, respondents were asked to provide their year-of-birth at the beginning of the CPS and again at the end of the CPS. • “Failed attention check” respondents, which means that the respondent failed to provide the correct answer to the attention check question in the survey (see the question cps21_attcheck).  • “Province Mismatch” respondents, who provided a different province of residence in the CPS compared with the PES.   A small portion of the remaining respondents have been flagged by less-severe data quality checks. As these responses may still be useful, they have been kept in the data. These were “Inattentive” respondents who took more than 60 minutes to complete the survey; “Initial duplicate” respondents, which means they took the survey again later, but that this was their initial response; “PES speeder” respondents, which means respondents were flagged as speeders in the PES but were retained. In the post-election survey, respondents who took less than one-third of the median time of the survey (7.12 minutes) were flagged as speeders. Speeders who took more than six minutes to complete the survey were retained but those who took less time were removed.   These respondents are identified by the variables: • cps21_duplicates_pid_flag • cps21_duplicate_ip_demo_flag • cps21_inattentive • pes21_speeder_low_quality • pes21_duplicates_pid_flag • pes21_inattentive  If these respondents are excluded, 19,009 Campaign Period Survey and 13,612 PostElection Survey responses remain. Campaign Period Survey (CPS)  Removal criteria   During the data cleaning process, respondents were categorized based on their most important reason for removal. In order to comply with that requirement while still retaining high data quality, the questionnaires made extensive use of "Don't know/ Prefer not to answer" options, where possible, and required respondents to respond in some way (even if only to select "Don't know/ Prefer not to answer"), except where otherwise noted (this was primarily noncategorical questions such as sliders). For example, some questions had the text "If you do not know, or prefer not to answer, please click ?" at the end of them. For those questions, if the respondent did not respond to that question or a component of that question (for example, did not click on or move a slider), then their response to that question, or that component of the question, was recorded as missing (-99).  The “back” button, represented by “?”, was available for respondents to go back to a previous question if they wished to change their answer. However, due to platform constraints, it is not possible to go back to a previous block. Due to the complex design of the survey, many small blocks of questions were used, making this a common restriction.   Variable naming   We have used descriptive names for variables, where possible, within character limits.   To help differentiate variables from the different waves of the 2021 CES, and to differentiate between variables from different years should one combine this dataset with others, we use the “cps21_” prefix for questions in the Campaign Period Survey, and the “pes21_” prefix for variables in the Post-Election Survey, where possible.   Metadata variables do not usually use these prefixes, except in cases where the same variable was used in both the Campaign Period Survey and the Post-Election Survey. Additional question modules   The Campaign Period Survey, and to a lesser extent the Post-Election Survey, included a number of additional question modules, which some respondents received. The majority of question modules were placed after the main survey, after the “additional demographics” block of questions.  Respondents were randomly assigned in a 3:1 ratio to different streams within the “CPS Modules” wave of the CPS. 72% of respondents were assigned to the “module” stream where they completed one or more of the additional question modules. 28% of the respondents were assigned to the “regular” stream where they completed additional core CPS questions instead of the question modules.   Respondents assigned to the “module” stream were then further randomly assigned to 1 of 10 module question streams. Depending on the assigned stream, respondents completed between 1 and 3 of the additional question modules. The streams were designed to balance the overall length of the survey and the number of questions, so that the length of the CPS was similar for all respondents.   In the PES, respondents were again randomly assigned to 1 of 10 module question streams. Depending on the stream assigned, respondents completed between 1 and 2 question modules. Some modules had specific sampling requirements.  These question modules are not included in the dataset nor discussed further in the codebook. These modules may be released by individual researchers and can be merged with this data by matching on cps21_ResponseID.  Campaign Period Survey   Meta Data for CPS   cps21_howvote cps21_howvote_EN1 cps21_howvote_FR1 cps21_howvote is a randomized embedded data field containing a flag indicating whether the respondent would receive a “0” or “1”. Some speeders close to the cutoff were retained.  • “Duplicate PID”- flags respondents that took the survey again later, but this was their initial response. Flagged by the respondent’s survey ID.   • “Inattentive” - respondents took more than 60 minutes to complete the survey.  To examine each quality flag individually, see the following variables: • pes21_speeder_low_quality - Retained speeders • pes21_duplicates_pid_flag - Duplicate PID (flagged by the respondent’s survey ID) • pes21_inattentive - Respondent took over 60 minutes to complete the PES. 1 Not included in dataset, to maintain respondent anonymity.   2 This included responses that initially had a match to the CPS, however, the corresponding CPS response was later removed due to a quality concern such as failing the attention check or providing a different province of residence in the CPS compared to the PES.  ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ 	 	 	 	 	 	 	 	 	o o o o  	o o  	 	 	 	  	 Voici certaines choses que les gens peuvent faire pour participer à la vie politique.
